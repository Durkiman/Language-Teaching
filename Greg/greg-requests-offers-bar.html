⏱ 8 min
The rule

Requests vs Offers

Requests

Asking someone to do something, or asking for something.

Can you help me?
Could I have the bill, please?

Offers

Offering to do something for someone.

Shall I bring you the menu?
Can I help you with that?

Quick test: Are you asking someone to help you? → Request. Are you the one offering to help? → Offer.
Politeness levels

How polite does it need to be?

All of these are correct — the right one depends on the situation.

CasualCan you pass the menu?
PoliteCould you pass the menu, please?
Very politeWould you mind passing the menu?
In your bar: "Could you…?" and "Could I have…?" are the safest default with customers — polite but not stiff.

⏱ 18 min
Free Production — 3-Scene Roleplay

Put it into practice

Three short scenes. Use polite requests and offers naturally in each — and remember to ask questions back where it makes sense.

Scene 1 — Borrowing You're short-staffed tonight. Ask a colleague if you can borrow something from behind their bar.
"Could I borrow…? Would it be okay if I…?"

Your teacher plays the colleague — negotiate a bit, don't just get an instant yes.
Scene 2 — Asking for help A customer is being difficult and you need backup. Ask a coworker for help.
"Could you help me with…? Can you deal with…?"

Explain briefly what the problem is before asking.
Scene 3 — Full customer interaction Your teacher is a customer at your bar tonight. Full interaction — greet them, take requests, make offers, handle at least one problem.
No script. Real conversation, start to finish.

Goal: use at least three different request/offer phrases naturally, with appropriate politeness throughout.
Teacher: Watch for missing "to" errors after modals (e.g. "Shall I to bring…") and overly blunt requests lacking politeness markers. Give feedback live and verbally after this section.
