Useful connectors

These small words make your English sound much more natural.

  • also — I work at a bar. I also teach yoga.
  • and — I like music and travel.
  • but — My English is okay, but I want to improve.
  • so — I meet a lot of tourists, so English is important for me.
⏱ 10 min
Read & Listen

Meet Alex

Read this short introduction. Then answer the questions below.

Hi! My name is Alex. I'm 34 years old and I'm from Lyon, in France. I live there now, but I grew up in a small town near the mountains.

I work as a chef in a restaurant. I love cooking — it's creative and I meet interesting people every day.

In my free time, I like to run, travel, and listen to music. I've been to Spain, Italy, and Morocco. I'd like to go to Japan one day — the food culture there is incredible.

I'm learning English because I want to talk with more customers at the restaurant — a lot of them are tourists. I also want to travel more and feel confident when I speak. My English is okay, but I want to improve!

Comprehension questions — answer out loud:

  • 1Where is Alex from?
  • 2What does he do for work?
  • 3What does he do in his free time?
  • 4Why is he learning English?
  • 5Which country does he want to visit?
Notice: Alex uses simple, clear sentences. He connects ideas with and, but, also, because. You can do exactly the same.

⏱ 15 min
Free Production

Say it — no notes

Tap each prompt to open it. Try to speak for at least 30 seconds. Fluency first — don't stop to correct yourself.

Warm-up Introduce yourself like you're meeting someone for the first time. Just your name and where you're from.
Try: "Hi, my name is… I'm from… It's nice to meet you."

Keep it simple. Smile. Make eye contact. This is the version you use every day.
Core You're at an international event and someone asks: "Tell me about yourself." Speak for 30–60 seconds.
Include: your name, where you're from, what you do, one hobby, and why you're learning English.

Challenge yourself: use also, but, because naturally in your answer.
Challenge A tourist walks into your workplace and starts a conversation. They say: "This is a great place — are you the owner?" — Continue the conversation for 2 minutes.
This is a roleplay. Your teacher plays the tourist.

Ideas to talk about: how long you've worked there, what you enjoy about it, where you'd recommend they visit next, whether you've been there yourself.

Focus on keeping the conversation going, not on being perfect.
Teacher: Take notes on errors during free production. Don't interrupt. Bring 2–3 key patterns to the cool-down.
⏱ 10 min
Conversation skill

Don't just answer — ask back

A conversation is not an interview. When someone asks you something, answer — then ask them something back. This keeps the conversation alive and makes you sound confident and natural.

The rule: Answer + ask back. Every time.
Question toolkit

Phrases to bounce it back

See the difference

Interview vs. conversation

Look at these two versions. Which one sounds more natural?

❌ Interview
Tourist: "Where are you from?"
You: "I'm from Brussels."
Tourist: "What do you do?"
You: "I work in a bar."
Tourist: "Do you travel much?"
You: "Yes, I like to travel."
✓ Conversation
Tourist: "Where are you from?"
You: "I'm from Brussels — have you been before?"
Tourist: "No, first time! What do you do?"
You: "I run a bar, actually — what brings you to the city?"
Tourist: "Just a weekend trip."
You: "Nice! Do you like trying local food? I can recommend a few places."
Roleplay

Keep it going

Your teacher starts the conversation. Your job: answer every question and ask something back. Try to keep it going for 3 minutes without stopping.

Round 1 You meet someone at a café. They say: "Hi! Is this seat taken?"
Say no, invite them to sit. Then ask where they're from or what they're doing in the city.

Rule: You must ask at least one question before the teacher asks the next one.
Round 2 An English-speaking customer sits at your bar and says: "Quiet night tonight, isn't it?"
Agree or disagree — then ask them something to start a real conversation.

Ideas: where they're from, how long they're in town, what they do, if they've tried local drinks.
Challenge Full 3-minute conversation — no topic given. Your teacher is a stranger. Keep it natural.
No script. No prompts. Just talk.

Goal: ask at least 4 questions during the conversation. Your teacher will count silently.
Teacher: Count the questions the student asks during the challenge round. Share the number at the end — "You asked 4 questions — great!" — then model any missed opportunities naturally.
