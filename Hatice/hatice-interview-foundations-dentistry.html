Core Phrase Bank

Tap a card to reveal how to use it. Have her repeat and personalize each one out loud immediately.
What really motivates me is...▾
Use to open an answer about motivation or "why".
"What really motivates me is helping people feel comfortable, even when they're afraid of the dentist."
One thing I've learned from... is...▾
Use to connect past experience to the role. Grammar: present perfect (have/has + past participle) — it links something from your past to who you are now, unlike past simple, which just reports a finished event.
"One thing I've learned from my studies is how important patience is with nervous patients."
If that happened, my first step would be to...▾
Use for hypothetical / "what would you do" questions.
"If that happened, my first step would be to stay calm and explain each step to the patient."
My background is in..., and along the way I...▾
Use to open the "tell me about your background" question — often asked first.
"My background is in dental studies, and along the way I discovered how much I enjoy working directly with patients."
What draws me to this position specifically is...▾
Use when asked "why this position / why here".
"What draws me to this position specifically is the chance to work directly with patients every day."
Something I'm actively working on is...▾
Use for weakness questions — honest but forward-looking.
"Something I'm actively working on is speaking more confidently in English during fast conversations."

The Answer Frame: Claim → Reason → Example

One simple shape for any answer, even ones you don't expect.
1
Claim
Say your main point in one short sentence.
2
Reason
Explain why, in one sentence.
3
Example
Give one short, concrete example or moment.
Grammar focus — second conditional (for "what would you do if...?")
Curveball questions like "What would you do if a patient was very anxious?" ask about an imagined situation, not a real one. Answer the same way:
If + past simple, ... + would + base verb
"If a patient was/were anxious, I would explain each step slowly."
"If I didn't know an answer, I would ask a colleague rather than guess."
Both "if I was" and "if I were" are heard in real interviews — "were" is the safer, slightly more formal choice.
Worked example — "Why do you want this position?"
Claim: "I want this position because it's the best next step for my career in dentistry."
Reason: "It combines direct patient contact with real clinical experience, which is exactly what I need right now."
Example: "For example, during my training I noticed I was most motivated on the days I worked directly with patients, not in the lab."
Worked example — curveball: "What would you do if a patient was very anxious or difficult?"
Claim: "I would stay calm and focus on making the patient feel safe first."
Reason: "Because anxious patients need reassurance before any procedure can go smoothly."
Example: "For example, I would explain each step slowly and check in with them before continuing."

Recovery Language

For when you blank, or catch yourself switching to Dutch.
You're living and studying between English and Dutch right now — it's completely normal for words to cross over under pressure. These phrases let you recover smoothly instead of panicking.
English/Dutch False Friends
Words that look similar but don't mean the same thing — easy to mix up under pressure.
practice / praktijk
NL sense "praktijk" = a clinic / office
EN meaning repeated training, or "in practice" = in reality
✓ "I want to gain more practice with patients." (not "I want to work in a practice" for a clinic)
eventually / eventueel
NL sense "eventueel" = possibly, maybe
EN meaning in the end, after some time
✓ "I eventually chose dentistry after trying other fields." (means: in the end — not "possibly")
actually / actueel
NL sense "actueel" = current, up to date
EN meaning in fact, really
✓ "Actually, I first became interested in dentistry at 16." (means: in fact — not "currently")
sensible / sensibel
NL sense "sensibel" = sensitive, easily affected
EN meaning reasonable, showing good judgment
✓ "It was a sensible decision to specialize early." (means: wise — not "emotionally sensitive")
to control / controleren
NL sense "controleren" = to check, to inspect
EN meaning to have power over, to direct
✓ "I checked the patient's chart" (not "I controlled the patient's chart")
