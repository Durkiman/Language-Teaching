## A1 — Beginner

*16 cards across 4 strands*

### Strand A: Who I am

#### A1-01 — Greetings & introducing yourself

- **Goal:** Can greet people, introduce him/herself and respond to introductions.
- **Functions:** greetings, giving personal information (first contact)
- **Grammar:** to be incl. questions and negatives
- **Exponents:** “Hi, how are you today?”; “Good evening”; “This is Mary. Pleased to meet you.”; “My name is…”; “I am from…”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Handle a first meeting: greet, introduce self (name, origin, where you live), respond to the other person's introduction, close politely.
- **Skin examples:** meeting a hotel receptionist, a fellow traveller, a new colleague

#### A1-02 — Exchanging personal information

- **Goal:** Can ask for and give personal information: address, nationality, age, family, job, hobbies.
- **Functions:** giving personal information
- **Grammar:** questions, present simple, 1st/2nd person
- **Lexis:** nationalities and countries, personal information
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Ask five personal questions and answer five, including one about family and one about work/hobbies; complete a hotel check-in or landing-card form.
- **Skin examples:** hotel check-in, meeting someone at a class, an online profile

#### A1-03 — My people & my things

- **Goal:** Can say what he/she has and talk simply about family and possessions.
- **Grammar:** have got, possessive adjectives, possessive 's, simple personal pronouns
- **Lexis:** topic: family life
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Present three people, three things: introduce three family members or friends and three possessions using have got and possessives correctly.
- **Skin examples:** family photo walk-through, what's in my bag, my home

#### A1-04 — Likes & dislikes

- **Goal:** Can say what he/she likes, loves and hates.
- **Grammar:** verb + -ing with like/love/hate
- **Lexis:** food and drink, hobbies
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Six true like/dislike sentences across two topics, at least three with verb + -ing.
- **Skin examples:** food likes for a trip, hobbies, music

### Strand B: My world

#### A1-05 — Daily life & routines

- **Goal:** Can describe habits, routines and daily life in simple sentences.
- **Functions:** describing habits and routines
- **Grammar:** present simple, all persons (3rd-person -s), adverbs of frequency: always, sometimes, never
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Describe a typical day in six or more sentences, including two other people (3rd person) and two frequency adverbs.
- **Skin examples:** a workday, a holiday morning, a weekend
- **Note:** First-contact version of A2-01.

#### A1-06 — What's happening now

- **Goal:** Can say what he/she and others are doing at the moment.
- **Grammar:** present continuous
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Picture narration: describe what five people in a busy scene are doing, correctly formed.
- **Skin examples:** a market scene, an airport, a beach

#### A1-07 — Where things are

- **Goal:** Can say and ask where things and places are.
- **Grammar:** there is/are incl. questions, prepositions of place, common prepositions
- **Lexis:** things in the town
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Describe a room or simple map (five sentences with there is/are + place prepositions), then answer two where-is questions.
- **Skin examples:** hotel room, own street, a café interior

#### A1-08 — Describing simply

- **Goal:** Can describe things, clothes and appearance with basic adjectives.
- **Grammar:** common and demonstrative adjectives, very basic intensifiers: very, really, comparatives and superlatives (first contact)
- **Lexis:** colours, dimensions, clothes
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Describe three items (one with this/that, one with colour + size, one with a comparative) so the listener can pick them out of a set.
- **Skin examples:** clothes shopping, describing luggage, souvenirs
- **Note:** Comparatives deepen at A2-20.

#### A1-09 — What I can do

- **Goal:** Can talk about abilities.
- **Grammar:** can/can't for ability
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Six true can/can't sentences about self and one other person; ask the listener two can-you questions.
- **Skin examples:** languages, sports, practical skills

### Strand C: Out in the world

#### A1-10 — Numbers & prices

- **Goal:** Can understand and use numbers, quantities and prices.
- **Functions:** understanding and using numbers, understanding and using prices
- **Grammar:** how much/how many with very common countable/uncountable nouns
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Ask the cost of three things, understand three spoken prices at varied speed, state two quantities about own life.
- **Skin examples:** hotel prices, market shopping, tickets

#### A1-11 — Time & dates

- **Goal:** Can tell the time, understand times and use days/months in arrangements.
- **Functions:** telling the time
- **Grammar:** prepositions of time: in/on/at
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Answer what-time for five clock times; say three true sentences about own schedule using in/on/at correctly.
- **Skin examples:** train departures, opening hours, meeting a friend

#### A1-12 — Finding the way

- **Goal:** Can ask for, understand and give simple directions.
- **Functions:** directions
- **Grammar:** imperatives (+/−), prepositions of place
- **Exponents:** “Go to the end of the street and turn right”; “It's straight ahead”; “The hotel is on the left”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Map task both directions: follow spoken directions to a target, then guide the teacher to a different target.
- **Skin examples:** finding the station, a pharmacy, the beach

#### A1-13 — Getting what you want

- **Goal:** Can ask for everyday goods and services: order, buy, and make simple requests in shops, cafés and travel situations.
- **Functions:** asking for goods and services
- **Grammar:** I'd like…, Can/Could I have…?
- **Lexis:** food and drink, things in the town, shops and shopping, travel and services, ways of travelling
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Two roleplays from the On Holiday scenario (e.g. café order + buying a ticket) without L1, including one I'd-like and one could-I-have.
- **Skin examples:** café in Spain, bus ticket, pharmacy
- **Note:** Full transactional card is A2-17.

### Strand D: Stretching out

#### A1-14 — First steps into the past

- **Goal:** Can make simple statements about past events and states.
- **Grammar:** past simple of to be, past simple, high-frequency verbs only
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Five true sentences about last weekend or a past holiday: two with was/were, three with other past verbs. No narrative structure required.
- **Skin examples:** last weekend, a past trip, yesterday evening
- **Note:** Narrative structure is A2-06.

#### A1-15 — First plans

- **Goal:** Can say what he/she is going to do.
- **Grammar:** going to (first contact)
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Three true going-to sentences about this week plus one going-to question to the listener.
- **Skin examples:** this evening, the next trip, the weekend
- **Note:** Full plans/arrangements work is A2-10.

#### A1-16 — Connecting ideas

- **Goal:** Can link words and simple sentences with basic connectors.
- **Discourse:** connecting words: and, but, because
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Speak or write a postcard with six sentences containing at least two and, two but, two because, all used correctly.
- **Skin examples:** a postcard from a trip, describing a friend, explaining a routine
- **Note:** This is the CEFR's own A1 coherence criterion.

## A2 — Elementary

*20 cards across 5 strands*

### Strand A: Describing the present

#### A2-01 — Habits & routines

- **Goal:** Can describe habits, routines and daily life in connected sentences.
- **Functions:** describing habits and routines
- **Grammar:** present simple, adverbs of frequency, adverbial phrases of time/frequency incl. word order
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Speak for 60 seconds about a typical week using at least four different frequency expressions, correctly placed.
- **Skin examples:** a gamer's daily schedule, a bar owner's opening routine, a DJ's weekend

#### A2-02 — Describing people

- **Goal:** Can describe a person's appearance and personality in simple terms.
- **Functions:** describing people
- **Grammar:** present simple, have got
- **Lexis:** adjectives: personality, description, feelings
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Describe two people the listener doesn't know (one appearance-focused, one personality-focused) so the listener can summarise them back.
- **Skin examples:** favourite footballer or anime character, a regular customer, a family member

#### A2-03 — Describing things & objects

- **Goal:** Can describe everyday objects: appearance, material, purpose.
- **Functions:** describing things
- **Grammar:** it's made of… / it's used for + -ing, -ed vs -ing adjectives
- **Lexis:** objects and rooms, colours, dimensions
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Mystery object: describe three objects without naming them (material, size, purpose each); listener guesses.
- **Skin examples:** gaming gear, bar equipment, studio kit

#### A2-04 — Describing a place

- **Goal:** Can give an extended simple description of a place: where it is, what it has, what it's known for.
- **Functions:** describing places
- **Grammar:** there is/are, articles with countable/uncountable nouns, prepositions of place
- **Lexis:** things in the town
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Describe a place the listener has never visited in 5–6 connected sentences: location, size, two features, one thing it's famous for.
- **Skin examples:** a World Cup host city, a surf town in Brazil, a festival site

#### A2-05 — Likes, dislikes & preferences

- **Goal:** Can explain what he/she likes or dislikes about something and express wants politely.
- **Grammar:** verb + -ing (like/love/hate/enjoy), gerund as subject, would like vs want, intensifiers: quite, so, a bit, really
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Compare two options giving two likes and two dislikes with reasons, then state a preference.
- **Skin examples:** two games, two travel destinations, two music genres

### Strand B: Talking about the past

#### A2-06 — Telling a simple past story

- **Goal:** Can describe past activities and personal experiences as a short connected narrative.
- **Functions:** describing past experiences and storytelling
- **Grammar:** past simple (regular + irregular), wh-questions in the past
- **Discourse:** sequential past-time linkers: first, then, after that, finally
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Tell a 90-second true story using at least three sequencing linkers, then answer two follow-up wh-questions.
- **Skin examples:** a match retelling, a memorable night at the bar, a first gig

#### A2-07 — Setting the scene

- **Goal:** Can distinguish background situation from main events when narrating.
- **Grammar:** past continuous vs past simple
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Retell an incident with at least two scene-setting past continuous clauses interrupted by past simple events.
- **Skin examples:** what was happening when the goal was scored, an eventful shift, equipment failure mid-set

#### A2-08 — How things used to be

- **Goal:** Can contrast past habits and states with the present.
- **Grammar:** used to (+/−/?)
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Then vs now: four sentences contrasting a past habit with present reality.
- **Skin examples:** life before smartphones, the bar before renovation, music before streaming

#### A2-09 — Life experiences (present perfect, first contact)

- **Goal:** Can ask and answer about life experiences without specifying when.
- **Grammar:** present perfect with ever/never/yet/just, been vs gone, for/since (recognition only)
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Have-you-ever exchange: ask and answer five experience questions, switching correctly to past simple follow-ups.
- **Skin examples:** ever been to a stadium?, ever surfed?, ever played abroad?
- **Note:** Full present perfect vs past simple contrast is B1-04.

### Strand C: Talking about the future

#### A2-10 — Plans & intentions

- **Goal:** Can describe plans and arrangements.
- **Functions:** arrangements / arranging to meet people
- **Grammar:** going to (intentions), present continuous for fixed arrangements
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Describe next week: three intentions (going to) and two fixed arrangements (present continuous), correctly distinguished.
- **Skin examples:** next tournament, next trip, weekend plans

#### A2-11 — Predictions & spontaneous decisions

- **Goal:** Can make simple predictions and express decisions made at the moment of speaking.
- **Grammar:** will vs going to (opinion/decision vs evidence-based prediction)
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** React to five prompt situations choosing will or going to appropriately; justify one choice.
- **Skin examples:** match predictions, weather for a trip, spontaneous offers

### Strand D: Social interaction

#### A2-12 — Suggestions & invitations

- **Goal:** Can make and respond to suggestions and invitations; can discuss what to do and where to go and arrange to meet.
- **Functions:** suggestions, invitations
- **Exponents:** “Shall we…?”; “Let's…”; “Why don't we…?”; “Do you fancy…?”; “Would you like to…?”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Consensus roleplay: plan an outing from three options; each side makes two suggestions and rejects one politely.
- **Skin examples:** plan a squad session, a night out, a day trip

#### A2-13 — Requests & offers

- **Goal:** Can make polite requests and offers and respond to them.
- **Functions:** requests, offers
- **Exponents:** “Can/Could you…?”; “Could I have…?”; “Shall I…?”; “Can I help?”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Three-scene roleplay: borrowing something, asking for help, offering help, with appropriate politeness in each.
- **Skin examples:** in-game trade, customer service moment, borrowing gear

#### A2-14 — Advice

- **Goal:** Can give and ask for simple advice.
- **Functions:** advice
- **Grammar:** should/shouldn't, you could try…
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Respond to three friend-problems with one should, one shouldn't and one could suggestion, each with a reason.
- **Skin examples:** advice to a new player, to a first-time visitor, to a beginner DJ

#### A2-15 — Obligation & necessity

- **Goal:** Can express what is necessary, obligatory or forbidden.
- **Functions:** obligation and necessity
- **Grammar:** must/mustn't, have to
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Explain the rules of something familiar: two must, two mustn't, two have to.
- **Skin examples:** game rules, bar rules, airport rules
- **Note:** Keep the must/have-to distinction light at A2; full contrast returns in B1-19.

#### A2-16 — Possibility

- **Goal:** Can express uncertainty about present and future situations.
- **Grammar:** might, may, possibly, probably, perhaps
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Speculate about three open questions using at least three different possibility expressions.
- **Skin examples:** tournament outcomes, next season, what a customer will order

### Strand E: Getting things done & connecting ideas

#### A2-17 — Shops, travel & services

- **Goal:** Can make simple transactions in shops and travel situations: goods, prices, tickets, directions, opening times.
- **Functions:** transactional exchanges
- **Grammar:** much/many/some/any/a lot of with countable/uncountable, determiners: enough, a few, none
- **Lexis:** things in the town, shops and shopping, travel and services
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Two back-to-back transactional roleplays (a shop and a travel situation) completed without L1 and without breakdown.
- **Skin examples:** buying merch at a match, at the airport, ordering stock

#### A2-18 — Purpose & reasons

- **Goal:** Can explain why he/she does something or wants something.
- **Grammar:** to + infinitive of purpose, verb + to + infinitive (want, hope, forget, decide), because clauses
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Give five why-answers about own life, each using a purpose infinitive or because clause, with five different reasons.
- **Skin examples:** why learn English, why open a bar, why make music

#### A2-19 — Real conditions & consequences

- **Goal:** Can express general truths and likely future consequences.
- **Grammar:** zero conditional, first conditional
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Produce three zero conditionals (facts/rules) and three first conditionals (real plans/warnings), correctly distinguished.
- **Skin examples:** game mechanics, business logic, weather and plans

#### A2-20 — Comparing

- **Goal:** Can compare people, places and things.
- **Grammar:** comparatives with than, superlatives with the definite article, much / a bit + comparative
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Rank three items best to worst with two comparative and two superlative sentences justifying the ranking.
- **Skin examples:** three brawlers, three cities, three tracks

## B1 — Intermediate

*21 cards across 6 strands*

### Strand A: Narrating the past

#### B1-01 — Full narrative

- **Goal:** Can tell a story or describe an experience as a connected, structured narrative with background, events and a point.
- **Functions:** describing experiences and events
- **Grammar:** past simple + past continuous woven together, past tense responses
- **Discourse:** sequential linkers extended: later, after that, finally, while, as soon as
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Tell a 2–3 minute story with a clear beginning, complication and ending; listener retells the main events in order.
- **Skin examples:** the night everything went wrong at a gig, a legendary match, the bar's opening night

#### B1-02 — Before the past

- **Goal:** Can make the order of past events explicit when narrating, including events that happened earlier.
- **Grammar:** past perfect
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Retell a story that needs at least three past perfect clauses to make the sequence clear.
- **Skin examples:** why the set crashed (what had been forgotten), how a booking went wrong, a plot summary

#### B1-03 — Past habits

- **Goal:** Can describe how life used to be, contrasting repeated past habits with the present.
- **Grammar:** used to (consolidated), would for repeated past actions, never used to / didn't use to
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** 90-second 'when I was younger' monologue mixing used to (states + habits) and would (habits only), with at least one correct negative.
- **Skin examples:** music before streaming, childhood summers, the neighbourhood ten years ago

#### B1-04 — Finished vs unfinished time

- **Goal:** Can talk about experiences and recent events, correctly switching between present perfect and past simple.
- **Grammar:** present perfect vs past simple (full contrast), just/yet/still/already, for/since productive
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Interview exchange: five experience questions, each answer opening in present perfect and continuing in past simple with details.
- **Skin examples:** places played, countries visited, clients served

#### B1-05 — Duration up to now

- **Goal:** Can say how long a situation or activity has been going on.
- **Grammar:** present perfect continuous, contrast with present perfect simple for results
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Answer how-long questions about five areas of own life, choosing continuous vs simple appropriately.
- **Skin examples:** how long producing music, running the bar, learning English

### Strand B: Reporting & passing on information

#### B1-06 — Reported speech

- **Goal:** Can report what someone said or asked.
- **Grammar:** reported speech across a range of tenses, reporting verbs: say/tell/ask/explain
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Report a short dialogue to a third person: two statements, one question and one explanation, with correct backshift and say/tell distinction.
- **Skin examples:** reporting a customer complaint, what the coach said, band gossip

#### B1-07 — Simple passive

- **Goal:** Can describe processes, events and general facts where the agent is unknown or unimportant.
- **Grammar:** simple passive, present and past, by + agent when needed
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Describe a process or report an event in six sentences, at least four passive, defending one active/passive choice when asked.
- **Skin examples:** how vinyl is made, how a cocktail is prepared, how a stadium was built

### Strand C: Opinions & discussion

#### B1-08 — Opinions with reasons

- **Goal:** Can express personal views and opinions on familiar topics and support them.
- **Functions:** expressing opinions
- **Grammar:** enough / too + adjective, broader intensifiers
- **Discourse:** fuller justification beyond because
- **Exponents:** “I think…”; “I don't think…”; “In my opinion…”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** One-minute opinion on a familiar topic with two distinct supporting reasons and one example.
- **Skin examples:** best era of a genre, tourism: good or bad?, school subjects that should exist

#### B1-09 — Agreeing & disagreeing politely

- **Goal:** Can agree and disagree with others, including softened disagreement, and hold his/her position in informal discussion.
- **Functions:** agreement and disagreement
- **Exponents:** “I think so too”; “Exactly”; “So do I / Neither do I”; “Well, actually…”; “I see what you mean, but…”; “I'm sorry, but I think you're wrong”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Ten-statement reaction round using at least six different exponents, including two softened disagreements; defend one disagreement for 30 seconds.
- **Skin examples:** hot takes about music, football opinions, food opinions

#### B1-10 — Reviewing & recommending

- **Goal:** Can summarise a film, book, show or piece of media and give an opinion about it with a recommendation.
- **Functions:** talking about films and books
- **Lexis:** topics: film, books, media
- **Exponents:** “It was set in…”; “It was about…”; “It had a happy ending”; “If you like X you'll like this”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** 90-second spoken review (what it is, what it's about, what worked, who should watch/hear it), then answer two detail questions.
- **Skin examples:** an album, a Netflix series, a One Piece arc

#### B1-11 — Linking ideas: cause, effect, contrast

- **Goal:** Can connect a series of points into linear, linked discourse using explicit logical markers.
- **Discourse:** however, therefore, on the other hand, on the one hand… on the other hand…
- **Lexis:** contrasting-opinion exponents
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Present both sides of a familiar dilemma in one minute using on-the-one-hand/on-the-other-hand plus two other logical markers, concluding with therefore/so.
- **Skin examples:** vinyl vs digital, working for yourself vs a boss, city vs countryside

### Strand D: Speculating & hypothesising

#### B1-12 — Imagining differently (present/future)

- **Goal:** Can talk about hypothetical or unlikely present and future situations.
- **Grammar:** second conditional
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Answer three what-would-you-do-if questions with full conditional sentences, then pose two of your own.
- **Skin examples:** if you could play any venue, if the bar had unlimited budget

#### B1-13 — Regrets & alternate pasts

- **Goal:** Can speculate about how past events could have been different.
- **Grammar:** third conditional
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Retell a real past decision and give two third-conditional alternatives.
- **Skin examples:** the gig you turned down, the match that got away, a travel near-miss

#### B1-14 — Deduction about the present

- **Goal:** Can draw logical conclusions about present situations from evidence.
- **Grammar:** must / can't for deduction, contrast with might/may/could for weaker certainty
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Given five clues, produce deductions graded correctly across must be / might be / can't be.
- **Skin examples:** whodunnit clues, guessing a track's decade, reading a customer

#### B1-15 — Judging & speculating about the past

- **Goal:** Can criticise past actions and speculate about what may have happened.
- **Grammar:** should have / shouldn't have (criticism), might have / could have (speculation)
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** React to three what-went-wrong scenarios with one should-have judgment and one might-have speculation each.
- **Skin examples:** post-match analysis, why the event flopped, the package that never arrived

### Strand E: Managing conversation

#### B1-16 — Opening & closing conversations

- **Goal:** Can initiate a conversation naturally and end it politely.
- **Functions:** initiating and closing conversation
- **Exponents:** “Excuse me, I wonder if you could help me”; “Guess what!”; “You'll never believe what I saw…”; “It's been nice talking to you”; “Sorry, I've got to go — see you later”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Three cold-start roleplays (stranger, acquaintance, service situation): open, sustain for one minute, close appropriately.
- **Skin examples:** networking at a festival, chatting to a customer, meeting another player

#### B1-17 — Keeping the conversation alive

- **Goal:** Can interrupt politely, change the topic, return to a dropped topic, and keep a conversation flowing with natural spoken markers.
- **Functions:** managing interaction: interrupting, changing topic, resuming
- **Discourse:** informal markers: Right. Really? Well, anyway… I know how you feel
- **Exponents:** “Sorry to interrupt, but…”; “Could I just say something?”; “By the way…”; “Anyway, as I was saying…”; “What were we talking about?”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Five-minute genuine conversation in which the student must interrupt once, change topic once and steer back once, reacting with at least four informal markers (teacher tallies).
- **Skin examples:** any free conversation — skins itself with the week's topic

#### B1-18 — Checking & clarifying

- **Goal:** Can check understanding in both directions and repair breakdowns without abandoning the message.
- **Functions:** checking understanding
- **Exponents:** “Do you mean…?”; “I'm sorry, did you say…?”; “I'm not sure I understand — are you saying that…?”; “Do you follow me?”
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Deliberate-obstacle task: reach full understanding of a slightly-too-complex explanation using at least three different clarification moves, then explain it back.
- **Skin examples:** decoding a venue's technical rider, a supplier's conditions, rules of an unfamiliar game

### Strand F: Coping in the real world

#### B1-19 — Problems & non-routine situations

- **Goal:** Can explain why something is a problem and deal with less routine situations (complaints, changes, obstacles) in shops, travel and services.
- **Functions:** explaining a problem, requesting resolution
- **Grammar:** must/have to, need to / needn't, ought to
- **Lexis:** travel and services upgraded: check in, departure lounge, debit or credit card
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Two escalation roleplays (wrong booking; faulty purchase): state the problem, explain why it matters, propose a resolution, respond to pushback.
- **Skin examples:** double-booked DJ slot, a delivery gone wrong, flight cancelled

#### B1-20 — Explaining how

- **Goal:** Can describe how to do something, giving detailed step-by-step instructions.
- **Grammar:** imperatives + sequencing, need to, purpose clauses: to / so that
- **Discourse:** instructional sequencing: first, next, once you've…, make sure…
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Teach the listener a real skill in 2–3 minutes; success measured by whether the listener could actually do it.
- **Skin examples:** beatmatching basics, pouring a proper pint, a yoga pose

#### B1-21 — Refining the future

- **Goal:** Can make predictions with graded confidence and describe what will be in progress at a future time.
- **Grammar:** will vs going to for prediction (consolidated), might/may/probably integrated, future continuous
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** This-time-next-year monologue: three graded predictions (certain/probable/possible, each marked linguistically) plus two future continuous sentences.
- **Skin examples:** where the scene is heading, the business in five years, next season's table

## B2 — Upper-Intermediate

*21 cards across 6 strands*

### Strand A: Narrating & describing with precision

#### B2-01 — Extended narrative with evaluation

- **Goal:** Can narrate a complex sequence of events, weaving in background, complication and personal evaluation of what happened.
- **Functions:** narrating with evaluation and comment
- **Grammar:** past perfect continuous, narrative tenses fully integrated: simple, continuous, perfect, perfect continuous
- **Discourse:** evaluative asides: what was interesting was…, the strange thing was…, looking back…
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Tell a 2–3 minute story that includes at least one past perfect continuous clause and two evaluative comments on the events, not just a report of them.
- **Skin examples:** a project that unravelled, a trip that went sideways, how a deal came together

#### B2-02 — Describing trends & change over time

- **Goal:** Can describe how something has changed over an extended period and explain the causes.
- **Functions:** describing change and development
- **Grammar:** present perfect vs present perfect continuous for change, used to / would / didn't use to consolidated, cause-result linking: as a result, due to, this meant that
- **Lexis:** trend language: rise, fall, gradually, dramatically, level off

## C1 — Advanced

*20 cards across 6 strands*

### Strand A: Extended narrative, description & synthesis

#### C1-01 — Narrating with full stylistic control

- **Goal:** Can narrate an extended, well-formed story that weaves narrative tenses, evaluative commentary and understatement without conscious effort.
- **Functions:** narrating with stylistic control

- **Discourse:** evaluative and reflective commentary woven through, not appended
- **Evidence (pass/fail task):** Tell a 3–4 minute story to an audience unfamiliar with the topic; a listener should be able to state both what happened and how the speaker felt about it without being told directly.
