Before you start

Session 1 — Discovery

This is your guide for the whole first session. You see everything — the student only sees what you screen-share. Work through each section in order; jot observations as you talk. At the end, generate a profile you can save.

Quick setup
Opening the session — say something like:
"Today is just about getting to know each other — no tests, no pressure. I'm going to ask you some questions, and I'd love to hear your answers in as much detail as you want."
"If I ask something in English and you're not sure of a word, just say it in your language — that's totally fine here."
Phase 1 · Speaking comfort

First Words

Low-stakes conversation to hear their natural language. You're not correcting anything — you're listening for fluency, range, and confidence.

Conversation starters — pick 2–3
"Tell me a little about yourself — where you're from, what you do, what you like."
"What did you do last weekend? Tell me three things."
"Is there a place you really want to visit one day? Why that place?"
"Tell me about something you're good at — anything at all."
"What's one thing that made you happy this week?"

Phase 3 · Proficiency

Level Check

Work through these indicators in sequence — the more you tick, the more precise your placement. You don't need to complete every probe; stop when the picture is clear.

🎙 Oral probes — use these live, listen and tick what you hear
A0–A1: "What's your name? How old are you? Where are you from?" — Can they answer at all?
A1–A2: "Tell me about your day today — morning to now." — Do they form simple past/present sentences?
A2–B1: "Tell me about a trip or holiday you've been on. What did you do?" — Can they narrate in past tense with some detail?
B1–B2: "Tell me about a time something went wrong. How did you handle it?" — Can they express opinions and explain cause/effect?
B2+: "What do you think is the biggest challenge facing your generation? Why?" — Can they argue a point, use hedging language, recover from errors?

Phase 4 · What hooks them

Interests & How They Learn

This shapes every topic you pick and how you present material. Let them discover their own preferences — don't lead with options yet.

Questions to ask first (open-ended, listen before showing options)
"Outside of language learning, what do you spend your time on? What are you into?"
"Is there a subject, hobby, or world you could talk about for hours?"
"When you've learned something new and it stuck — what made it stick?"
"Do you prefer working with visuals, listening to things, doing exercises, or just talking?"
