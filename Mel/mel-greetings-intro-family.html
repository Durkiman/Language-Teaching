Grammar moment — jam hiding inside greetings
"Si je?" and "Mirë, ..." both come from the verb jam (to be). "Je" = you are. You'll meet the full verb properly in the next section, but notice it's already working for you right here.

Family note: they'll often say asht instead of the standard është — same meaning, Gheg dialect.

👪 Family & kam
0:00
Why this matters to you

This is no longer an abstract vocabulary list — these are Durim's actual nana, baba, motra, vëllau. Naming them correctly, and being able to say what you have (kam), is how you start to feel like part of the family instead of a guest.

Full paradigm — kam (to have)
WhoAlbanianEnglishFrench
Unë (I)kamI havej'ai
Ti (you)keyou havetu as
Ai/Ajo (he/she)kahe/she hasil/elle a
Ne (we)kemi (kena)we havenous avons
Ju (you pl.)keniyou all havevous avez
Ata/Ato (they)kanëthey haveils/elles ont
Examples: Kam një motër. (I have a sister.) · Ai ka dy vëllezër. (He has two brothers.)
Possessives — first contact: im/ime, yt/jote
The ending changes with the noun's gender, not the owner's:

im (my, masc. noun) — babai im (my dad)
ime (my, fem. noun) — nana ime (my mom)
yt (your, masc. noun) — vëllau yt (your brother)
jote (your, fem. noun) — motra jote (your sister)

Task 3 · The family photo

Grab a real photo of Durim's family. Point to six people, name each with the correct kinship term, and say one true kam-sentence about each one.

Për secilin person: "Kjo është ... (kinship term). Ai/Ajo ka ..."
For each person: "This is ... (kinship term). He/She has ..."
💡 Example: "Kjo është halla. Ajo ka dy fëmijë." (This is my aunt. She has two children.)
