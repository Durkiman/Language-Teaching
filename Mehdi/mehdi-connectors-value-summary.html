Presentation · 10 min
What a Value Summary Looks Like
Read this example — written by a coach about their client. Notice the three parts: Problem → Transformation → Outcome. This is the structure you will use.
Model Text · A Coach's Value Summary
① Problem
Many entrepreneurs know what they want to build — but they don't know where to start. They feel overwhelmed by choices, they doubt themselves, and they lose time on the wrong priorities. Without the right support, many give up before they even begin.
🔍 Notice: the problem is described from the client's experience — not from the coach's perspective. "They feel… / They doubt… / They lose…"
② Transformation
Through our work together, I help clients build clarity about their business idea, identify their real strengths, and create a step-by-step plan they can actually follow. We work on both the practical side — strategy, structure, finances — and the personal side — confidence, motivation, and mindset.
🔍 Notice: "Through our work together…" — this is the key connector that opens the transformation section.
③ Outcome
As a result, my clients leave our sessions with a clear direction, real tools, and the confidence to take action. They don't just understand their business better — they believe in it. That is the difference between knowing and doing.
🔍 Notice: "As a result…" opens the outcome. The final sentence is a strong closing line — emotional and memorable.
Quick Check — Answer Orally
1. What is the client's problem in this summary?
Say it in your own words — don't read from the text.
2. What two things does the coach work on with the client?
There are two sides mentioned — practical and…?
3. What is the difference between the transformation and the outcome?
Which part is about the process and which is about the result?
Connectors · 7 min
The Language of the Summary
These are the phrases that hold a value summary together. You will use them in your writing. Study them — then close the page and write.
① Problem — How to Open
Many people feel…
Beaucoup de gens se sentent…
"Many people feel lost when they start a new project."
The main challenge is…
Le principal défi est…
"The main challenge is knowing where to begin."
Without support, people often…
Sans soutien, les gens ont souvent tendance à…
"Without support, people often give up too early."
Most of my clients come to me because…
La plupart de mes clients viennent me voir parce que…
"…because they feel stuck and don't know why."
② Transformation — How to Describe the Work
Through our work together…
À travers notre travail ensemble…
"Through our work together, I help clients find clarity."
I help clients to…
J'aide mes clients à…
"I help clients to reconnect with their body and breath."
We work on both… and…
Nous travaillons à la fois sur… et sur…
"We work on both the physical and emotional side."
Step by step…
Étape par étape…
"Step by step, the client learns to regulate their stress."
③ Outcome — How to Close
As a result…
En conséquence / Par conséquent…
"As a result, clients feel calmer and more focused."
My clients leave with…
Mes clients repartent avec…
"My clients leave with real tools they can use every day."
The difference is…
La différence, c'est…
"The difference is they now have a practice, not just knowledge."
They don't just… — they…
Ils ne font pas que… — ils…
"They don't just feel better — they understand why."
💡 Key pattern: Problem uses "feel / often / because" — Transformation uses "through / help / work on" — Outcome uses "as a result / leave with / difference". One connector per section is enough.

Free Production · 10 min
Present It Out Loud
You have written the summary. Now you present it — first as a reading, then as a conversation. Fluency first. No stopping for errors.
Step 1 · Read Your Summary Aloud
→
Read your completed summary from the previous section — out loud, clearly, as if presenting to a real client.
Focus on pace and confidence — not perfection. This is a professional delivery, not a test.
Step 2 · Client Follow-up Questions
The teacher plays Durim as a client. After Mehdi reads the summary, the teacher asks these questions. Mehdi answers without reading — speaking freely.
1
"That sounds interesting — but what does breathwork actually mean? Can you explain it simply?"
→ Tests whether Mehdi can paraphrase what he heard — not just recite the summary
2
"How is this different from meditation or yoga?"
→ Pushes him to compare and contrast — good practice for the comparative grammar too
3
"Who is this for — is it for people with serious problems, or can anyone do it?"
→ Forces him to describe the ideal client in his own words
💡 Tip: Question 2 is a great natural entry point for comparative language — "Breathwork is more physical than meditation… / It's faster than therapy…". If it comes up naturally, let it. If not, note it for next session.
