Presentation · 10 min
When a Client Pushes Back
Read the text carefully — tap highlighted phrases. Notice how the coach explains, justifies, and resolves the objection.
Reading Text
A client says: "It's too expensive." Many coaches freezefreeze = stop, don't know what to say — se figer in this moment. But an objection is not a rejection — it is a request for more informationThe client usually wants clarity, not a lower price.

The first thing a coach needs toneed to = necessity — la nécessité de do is understand what "expensive" really means to this client. You don't have todon't have to = it's not necessary — pas besoin de (absence of obligation, not prohibition) defend your price immediately. You ought toought to = advice, recommendation — devrait, il serait bon de ask a question first: "Compared to what?"

A good coach mustmust = strong obligation, often from the speaker's own conviction stay calm and curious, not defensive. You have tohave to = obligation from outside, external necessity remember: the client's objection is about their fear, not your value.

Once you understand the real concern, you ought toSuggests the recommended next step reconnect them to the transformation, not the price. You needn'tneedn't = formal way to say "don't need to" — used in British English lower your price to close the sale — you need to help them see clearly what they are actually buying.
💡 Key idea: An objection is a request for clarity. State the problem, explain why it matters, propose a resolution, respond calmly to pushback.

Grammar · 10 min
Must, Need To, Ought To
Three ways to talk about obligation and advice — the exact language for explaining a problem and proposing a resolution.
Must / Have to Obligation — something is necessary
Subject must / have to + verb
Must
Obligation from the speaker's own belief or conviction
"A coach must stay calm."
Have to
Obligation from outside — a rule, a situation
"You have to remember: it's about their fear."
Negative: don't have to = not necessary (NOT the same as "must not")
Need to / Needn't Necessity — and the absence of it
Subject need to / needn't + verb
Need to
Something is necessary — practical, not emotional
"You need to understand the real concern."
Needn't
Not necessary — formal, common in British English
"You needn't lower your price."
Ought to Advice, recommendation — the wise thing to do
Subject ought to + verb
Recommendation
Similar to "should" — but slightly more formal
"You ought to ask a question first."
Wise next step
What a good coach would do in this situation
"You ought to reconnect them to the transformation."
🔁 Side by Side — Choosing the Right One
FormMeaningExample
muststrong obligation, internal"I must stay calm."
have toobligation, external / situational"I have to answer objections carefully."
need topractical necessity"I need to ask more questions."
don't have to / needn'tnot necessary"You don't have to lower your price."
ought toadvice, recommendation"You ought to listen first."
💡 Quick test: Which form fits — a rule you believe in, or good advice? "A coach ___ stay calm" → must. "You ___ try this technique" → ought to.

Escalation Roleplay · 12 min
Handle the Objection
Two escalation roleplays. For each: state the problem, explain why it matters, propose a resolution, respond to pushback. Use today's grammar.
Client · Teacher plays this Roleplay 1 — Price Objection
"I like what you're offering, but honestly, it's more expensive than I expected. I'm not sure I can justify it."
💡 Push back once more after his first response — make him work through the full sequence.
Mehdi · Coach Your Task — 4 Steps
  • 1. Understand — ask what "expensive" means to them. Don't defend yet.
  • 2. Explain — say why the price reflects the value, using need to / have to.
  • 3. Propose — offer a resolution: a call, a trial session, a payment plan — using ought to.
  • 4. Respond to pushback — stay calm when they push back again.
Client · Teacher plays this Roleplay 2 — Timing Objection
"This all sounds great, but I don't think now is the right time for me. Maybe in a few months?"
💡 A softer objection — good contrast to Roleplay 1's direct price pushback.
Mehdi · Coach Your Task — Same 4 Steps
  • 1. Understand — what's really behind "not the right time"?
  • 2. Explain — why waiting might cost more than starting now.
  • 3. Propose — a smaller first step, not the full commitment.
  • 4. Respond to pushback — respect their choice without giving up too early.
