05Reading — Two Texts
🎤 TEXT A — Teacher reads aloud · Micha listens
A Paradise Passed Between Empires
Long before tourists arrived with their superyachts and sunglasses, Saint-Barthélemy belonged to a very different world. The island's first known inhabitants were the Arawak people, who settled its green, rocky hills thousands of years ago. Christopher Columbus spotted the island in 1493 and named it after his brother Bartolomeo — giving it the melodic name we still use today.

For two centuries, the island changed hands repeatedly. The French took control in 1648, but pirates made the waters around it treacherous and dangerous. The buccaneers of the Caribbean — fearless, ruthless, and free — used the island's hidden coves to hide their ships and divide their treasure.

Then, in a remarkable deal, France sold the island to Sweden in 1784 in exchange for trading rights in Gothenburg. Sweden renamed the capital Gustavia after King Gustav III, built the imposing Fort Karl, and turned it into a prosperous free port. For 94 years, this tiny Caribbean island flew the Swedish flag.
📖 TEXT B — Micha reads
The Return to France & the Island Today
In 1878, Sweden held a vote — and the people of Saint-Barths chose to return to France. Sweden sold the island back for 320,000 francs. The Swedish influence, however, never disappeared completely. Even today, the capital Gustavia still has distinctive red Swedish-style rooftops, and the island's coat of arms includes a Nordic symbol.

Today, Saint-Barthélemy is one of the most exclusive holiday destinations in the world. Its pristine beaches, crystal-clear lagoons, and elegant French restaurants attract celebrities, billionaires, and travellers from all over the globe. Yet the island has kept something rare — a sense of quiet, authentic beauty that few Caribbean islands still have.

With a population of just 10,000 people, Saint-Barths remains a place where history and luxury coexist. Pirates once sailed past these same turquoise waters. Swedish soldiers once stood guard on these same sun-drenched hills. And today, Micha's teacher walked on the same white, powdery sand.
