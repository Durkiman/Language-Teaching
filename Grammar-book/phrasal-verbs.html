Phrasal Verbs

Separable & Inseparable

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

A phrasal verb is a verb + a particle (a preposition and/or adverb) that together create a new meaning, often different from the individual words. Phrasal verbs can be transitive (take an object) or intransitive (no object), and transitive ones can be separable or inseparable.

Type Rule Example
Separable object can go between verb and particle; must if it's a pronoun Turn off the light. / Turn it off.
Inseparable object always after the particle Look after the kids. / Look after them.
Intransitive no object at all The plane took off.
Golden rule with pronouns: with separable phrasal verbs, a pronoun object (it, him, her, them...) MUST go between the verb and particle — "Turn it off," never "Turn off it."

Register

💬 Everyday/spoken
find out, put off, give up, look into.
📄 Formal equivalent
discover, postpone, abandon, investigate.
⚡ Same particle, different meaning
give up (quit)

She gave up smoking.

give up (hand over)

He gave up his seat to her.

take off (remove)

He took off his shoes.

take off (leave the ground)

The plane took off on time.

break down (stop working)

The generator broke down mid-show.

break down (become upset)

He broke down in tears.

Three-word phrasal verbs (verb + adverb + preposition) are always inseparable: "I'm looking forward to the trip," "She came up with a plan."

Particle patterns — what the particle usually adds

Particles aren't random — the same one often adds a similar shade of meaning across many different verbs. Recognising the pattern helps you guess the meaning of a phrasal verb you've never seen before.

Particle Usual pattern Examples
upcompletion or increasewrap up, fill up, speak up, heat up
outdiscovery, or something disappearingfigure out, sort out, run out, cut out
downreduction or stoppingwind down, calm down, break down, turn down
offseparation or departureset off, head off, cut off, drop off
oncontinuationcarry on, keep on, hold on, go on
backreturn or reversalturn back, get back, pay back, look back
These are tendencies, not laws. "Look up" (find information) doesn't fit "completion/increase" neatly — always check meaning with a dictionary or context, especially for idiomatic (non-guessable) phrasal verbs.
