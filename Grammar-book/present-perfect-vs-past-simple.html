The Present Perfect

vs. Past Simple

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

Formation

The present perfect is built with have/has + past participle. Regular past participles end in -ed (the same as the past simple); many common verbs are irregular (go → gone, see → seen).

Form Structure Example
Affirmative subject + have/has + past participle She has finished her homework. / I have finished mine.
Negative subject + haven't/hasn't + past participle She hasn't finished yet.
Question Have/Has + subject + past participle ? Has she finished yet?

When do we use it?

🌟 Life experience
I have visited Japan twice.
⏳ Unfinished time period
She has called me three times today.
📰 Recent news with a present result
They have just landed at the airport.
📏 Duration up to now (for/since)
We have lived here since 2015.
⚡ The key question: is the time period finished or not?
Present Perfect

No specific time is given, or the time period is still open (this week, so far, in my life).

I have seen that film. / I have finished my work this morning. (it's still morning)

Signal words: ever, never, already, just, yet, since, for, so far, recently

Past Simple

A specific, finished time is given or understood (yesterday, last year, in 2020).

I saw that film last night. / I finished my work this morning. (it's now afternoon)

Signal words: yesterday, last week/year, in [year], ago, when I was young

For vs. since — both pair with the present perfect
for + length of time

for two years, for a month, for ages

since + starting point

since 2019, since Monday, since I was a child

I've lived here for six years. / I've lived here since 2019.
