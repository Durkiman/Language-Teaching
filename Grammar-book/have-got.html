Have Got

Possession, Description & Informal "Have"

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

Have got means exactly the same as have — it's the everyday spoken British English way of talking about possession, relationships, physical features and illnesses. There's no auxiliary "do": got itself moves to make negatives and questions, just like a modal verb.

possession
She's got a new bike.
relationships
They've got two children.
physical features
He's got blue eyes and short hair.
illness / feelings
I've got a headache.
Careful: "have got" ≠ "have got to". I've got a car = possession. I've got to go = I have to go (obligation) — a completely different meaning hiding inside a similar-looking sentence.

LEVEL A2 · Elementary
Exercise 4 — True or false? Read, then mark T or F.

My friend Lena has got a big family. She's got three brothers but she hasn't got any sisters. Her parents have got a small farm outside the city — they've got chickens, two horses and a very old dog. Lena hasn't got a car yet, so she's got a bicycle instead. She says she's got everything she needs.

  1. Lena has got three brothers.
  2. Lena has got two sisters.
  3. Her parents have got chickens and horses.
  4. Lena has got a car.
  5. Lena has got a bicycle.
Exercise 5 — Write negatives (1, 3) and questions (2, 4).
  1. I (have/not) a pet.
  2. you (have) any brothers or sisters?
  3. She (have/not) a driving licence.
  4. he (have) a job?
Exercise 6 — Reorder the words to make correct sentences.
  1. got / a / I've / dog
  2. brothers / got / any / hasn't / she
  3. car / got / new / a / they've
  4. eyes / got / he's / blue
  5. got / you / a / have / pet?

LEVEL B2 · Upper-Intermediate
Exercise 10 — Read, then mark each statement T or F.

The flat has got three bedrooms, although the smallest one hasn't got a window, so the agency can't legally call it a bedroom on the listing. It's got a small balcony and, unusually for this price, it's got underfloor heating throughout. The previous tenants have got a dog, so the landlord has got to have the carpets professionally cleaned before the new tenants move in. Anyone interested has got until Friday to submit an offer.

  1. The flat has got three legally-recognised bedrooms.
  2. The flat has got a balcony.
  3. The flat hasn't got underfloor heating.
  4. The landlord has got to clean the carpets before new tenants move in.
  5. Interested people have got until Friday to make an offer.

Exercise 12 — Guided writing.

Describe a person you know well (appearance, family, and one or two obligations they've got this week) in 6–8 sentences. Use have got for possession/description and have got to at least once for obligation.

LEVEL C2 · Proficiency
Exercise 16 — Open writing.

In 80–120 words, write an informal message to a friend describing your new flatmate — appearance, personality, and one idiomatic use of "got" (e.g. hasn't got a clue, got what it takes). Then rewrite the same description in a formal register using plain "have" throughout.

Exercise 17 — Reorder into formal, complex sentences (using plain "have", not "have got").

  2. He's got to finishing the audit by Monday.
