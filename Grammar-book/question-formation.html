Question Formation

Word Order & Question Words

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

Question typeWord orderExample
Yes/Noauxiliary + subject + verbDo you like coffee?
Wh-questionWh-word + auxiliary + subject + verbWhere do you live?
Subject questionWh-word + verb (no auxiliary)Who called you?
Indirect questions keep statement word order, with no auxiliary inversion: "Do you know where she lives?" (not "where does she live").

Exercise 9 — Multiple choice: subject vs object questions.
  1. "___ broke the window?" a) Who  b) Whom
  2. "___ did you call?" a) Who  b) Whom
  3. Which needs "did"? a) "Who called you?"  b) "Who did you call?"
  4. Subject question about "Someone ate the cake": a) Who ate the cake?  b) Who did eat the cake?
  5. "How many students ___ in the class?" a) are there  b) there are
  6. Indirect question: "Do you know ___ she lives?" a) where  b) where does
LEVEL B2 · Upper-Intermediate
Exercise 10 — Read, then mark each statement T or F.

During the panel discussion, the moderator asked several probing questions, including why the policy had taken so long to implement and who, ultimately, would be held accountable if it failed. One panelist, visibly uncomfortable, asked whether the moderator could clarify what exactly "accountability" meant in this context. Another audience member wanted to know how the new measures would differ from previous attempts, and whether any independent body would be monitoring compliance.

  1. The moderator asked why the policy took so long to implement. ___
  2. Nobody asked about accountability. ___
  3. A panelist requested clarification on a term used. ___
  4. An audience member asked about independent monitoring. ___
  5. The audience member's question was about differences from previous attempts. ___

LEVEL C1 · Advanced
Exercise 13 — Rewrite each question in a more formal register.
  1. So, what's the deal with the new policy?
  2. Why'd they scrap the whole plan?
  3. Who's gonna be in charge of this?
  4. What's up with the delay?
  5. D'you reckon it'll work?
Exercise 14 — Multiple choice: what does this question type express?
  1. "Isn't that exactly what we predicted?" a) surprise/a rhetorical point, not real doubt  b) genuine uncertainty
  2. "You do realize the risks, don't you?" a) seeks confirmation of something believed true  b) a genuine yes/no question
  3. "Why on earth would anyone agree to that?" a) rhetorical disbelief  b) a literal request for a reason
  4. "Could I possibly trouble you for a moment?" a) formal and deferential  b) blunt
  5. "Isn't it wonderful?" a) invites agreement  b) expresses doubt
  6. "Who would have thought?" a) rhetorical surprise  b) a real request for an answer
Exercise 15 — Correct the mistakes in these complex sentences.
  1. She asked me did I want to come.
  2. I wonder why does he always arrive late.
  3. Could you tell me what time does the train leave?
  4. He asked her where was she going.
  5. Do you know how much does the ticket costs?
