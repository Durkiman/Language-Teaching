Wish & If Only

Regrets, Hopes & Annoyance

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

Wish has an everyday, formulaic use (good wishes) and a grammatical use (wanting reality to be different). If only shares the grammatical use, but sounds more emphatic.

wish + noun
everyday good wishes: "I wish you luck."
wish + past simple
want the present to be different: "I wish I had more time."
wish + past perfect
regret about the past: "I wish I had studied."
wish + would
annoyance at someone/something else: "I wish you would listen."
Careful: never "wish + will." "I wish I will come" is wrong. For present/future wants, use could: "I wish I could come."
Careful: "wish + would" is (almost) never for yourself. "I wish I would have a car" is a common mistake — say "I wish I had a car." Save wish + would for complaining about someone else's behaviour: "I wish he would stop interrupting."
🔁 Wish vs If Only

Grammatically identical (same tenses), but if only is more dramatic and often stands alone, without a main clause: "If only I had known!" — where wish usually needs a subject + wish + clause: "I wish I had known."

📊 2. Reference Tables

The four forms at a glance

SituationStructureExample
everyday good wishwish + object + nounI wish you a safe trip.
present unrealwish + past simpleI wish I lived closer.
past regretwish + past perfectI wish I had left earlier.
annoyance at otherswish + would + base verbI wish it would stop raining.

Was vs were after "wish"

RegisterFormExample

LEVEL A2 · Elementary (Hope vs Wish)
Exercise 4 — True or false? "Hope" is for realistic things; "wish" (in this exercise) is for good wishes.

Before the race, Ana said "I hope you win!" to her brother. After the race, she said "I wish you had won" because he came second. Their mother texted "I wish you both a great day" that morning, before the race even started.

  1. Ana said "I hope" before the race started.
  2. Her brother won the race.
  3. The mother's message was a good wish, not a regret.
Exercise 5 — Choose "hope" or "wish" for each sentence.
  1. I you a wonderful holiday. (good wish, before it happens)
  2. I the weather is nice tomorrow. (realistic, possible)
  3. I your exam goes well. (realistic, possible)
Exercise 6 — Reorder the words to make correct wishes.
  1. luck / you / good / I / wish
  2. a / trip / you / safe / wish / I
  3. success / every / you / wish / we

Exercise 9 — Multiple choice: was or were?

LEVEL B2 · Upper-Intermediate (Past Regret & If Only)
Exercise 10 — Read, then mark each statement T or F.

Tom missed the last train. "If only I had left the party earlier," he said. He wishes he had checked the timetable before leaving. Now he has to wait two hours for the next one, and he wishes he had brought a book to read.

  1. Tom caught the last train.
  2. He regrets not leaving the party sooner.
  3. He checked the timetable before leaving.
  4. He has a book with him to pass the time.
Exercise 11 — Find and correct the (subtler) mistake in each sentence.
  1. I wish I would have studied harder.
  2. If only I would have known that earlier.
  3. She wishes she didn't missed the flight.
  4. He wishes he has apologised sooner.
Exercise 12 — Guided writing.

Write about three regrets — one about now, two about the past — in 4–6 sentences. Use wish + past simple, wish + past perfect, and if only at least once each.

LEVEL C1 · Advanced (Wish + Would — Annoyance)

  2. She never replies to my messages. It's annoying.
  3. It won't stop raining.
  4. My neighbours play loud music every night.
Exercise 14 — Multiple choice: is "wish + would" used correctly?
  1. "I wish you would listen to me."
  2. "I wish I would have a bigger flat."
  3. "I wish the bus would come."
Exercise 15 — Correct the mistakes in these complex sentences.
  1. I wish I would earn more, but wishing doesn't change it.
  2. If only he would have listened, none of this would have happened.
  3. She wishes she would be more confident in meetings.
  4. We wish they would stopped complaining about the schedule.
LEVEL C2 · Proficiency (Mixed Forms & Register)
Exercise 16 — Open writing.

In 80–120 words, write a short reflective passage mixing at least four of: everyday wish, wish + past simple, wish + past perfect, wish + would, and if only.

Exercise 17 — Reorder into natural, idiomatic sentences.

Exercise 18 — Correct the nuanced errors.
