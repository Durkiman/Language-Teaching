Conditionals

Zero, First, Second & Third

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

Conditional sentences have two parts: an if-clause (the condition) and a main clause (the result). English has four common types, each for a different kind of situation.

Type If-clause Main clause Use
Zero present simple present simple General truths / facts
First present simple will + base verb Real, possible future
Second past simple would + base verb Unreal / hypothetical present
Third past perfect would have + past participle Unreal past (didn't happen)
🌍 Zero
If you heat ice, it melts.
🔜 First
If it rains, we will stay home.
💭 Second
If I had more time, I would travel.
⏪ Third
If I had studied, I would have passed.
Two extra notes: in formal second conditionals, use were for all subjects (If I were you…). "Could" or "might" can replace "would" to show ability or possibility instead of certainty (If I had time, I could help).

18 exercises across all six CEFR levels — three per level, increasing in difficulty and moving from zero/first towards second/third conditionals. Answers on the last page.

LEVEL A1 · Beginner (Zero Conditional)
Exercise 1 — Multiple choice: circle the correct form.
  1. If you heat water, it (boil / boils).
  2. If people don't eat, they (get / gets) hungry.
  3. If it snows, the ground (turn / turns) white.
  4. Plants die if they (not/get) water.
  5. If you touch fire, you (get / gets) burned.
  6. Metal expands if you (heat / heats) it.
Exercise 2 — Matching: match each cause (1–4) to its effect (a–d).
1. If you mix red and blue, ___
a. it melts.
2. If you don't sleep enough, ___
b. you get purple.
3. If ice gets warm, ___
c. they die.
4. If plants don't get sunlight, ___
d. you feel tired.
Exercise 3 — Fill in the blank: complete each zero conditional.
  1. If you ______ (boil) water, it ______ (evaporate).
  2. Ice ______ (melt) if it ______ (get) warm.
  3. If children ______ (not/eat) vegetables, they ______ (miss) important vitamins.
  4. Plants ______ (grow) if they ______ (get) enough light.
LEVEL A2 · Elementary (First Conditional)
Exercise 4 — True or false? Read, then mark T or F.

  1. If it rains, the picnic will happen anyway. ___
  2. They will go to the park if the weather is nice. ___
  3. They won't bring food if they go to the park. ___
  4. If everyone comes, they will need more chairs. ___
  5. Some people might sit on the grass. ___
Exercise 5 — Complete each first conditional sentence.
  1. If she ______ (not/study), she ______ (not/pass) the exam.
  2. ___ you ___ (come) if I ___ (invite) you?
  3. If it ______ (not/rain), we ______ (go) outside.
  4. What ______ you ______ (do) if you ______ (win) the lottery?

LEVEL B2 · Upper-Intermediate (Second & Third)
Exercise 10 — Read, then mark each statement T or F.

If Maria had accepted the job offer last year, she would be living in London now. She sometimes wonders what her life would look like if she had taken that risk. If she took the job now, she would have to relocate immediately, which isn't realistic with two young children. Her husband believes that if they had more support nearby, she would reconsider. For now, she tells herself that if the right opportunity came along, she would seriously consider it.

  1. Maria accepted the job offer last year. ___
  2. She would be living in London now if she had accepted it. ___
  3. She could relocate immediately without any difficulty. ___
  4. Her husband thinks more support would change her decision. ___
  5. She has completely ruled out ever taking a similar job. ___

Write about a decision you regret, or a hypothetical scenario, in at least 5 sentences. Use the second conditional for a present hypothetical and the third conditional for a past hypothetical. Include at least one negative.

LEVEL C1 · Advanced (Third & Formal Inversion)
Exercise 13 — Rewrite each sentence in a more formal register, using inversion where possible.
  1. If I'd known, I would've said something.
  2. If we had more funding, we could expand faster.
  3. If they hadn't intervened, the situation would've gotten worse.
  4. If she wins the award, she'll be thrilled.
  5. If you need anything, just call.
Exercise 14 — Multiple choice: identify the conditional type or its formal equivalent.
  1. "If she had studied medicine, she would be a doctor now." a) a mixed conditional (past condition, present result)  b) a standard third conditional
  2. "If he weren't so stubborn, he wouldn't have lost the deal." a) a mixed conditional (present condition, past result)  b) a standard second conditional
  3. "If I had taken that job, I would be earning more now." a) mixed  b) standard third
  4. "Had I known, I would have called." — this inversion replaces: a) "If I had known"  b) "If I know"
  5. "Were she to apply, she would surely be accepted." — this replaces: a) "If she applies"  b) "If she applied"
  6. "Should you have any questions, don't hesitate to ask." — this replaces: a) "If you have any questions"  b) "If you had any questions"
Exercise 15 — Correct the mistakes in these complex conditional sentences.
  1. Had she not missed the flight, she would arrived on time.
  2. If the treaty hadn't been signed, tensions would has escalated further.
  3. Were the funding cut, the project will collapse.
  4. Should the board rejects the proposal, we will have to reconsider our strategy.
  5. If the CEO had listened to advisors, the company wouldn't losing so much money now.
LEVEL C2 · Proficiency (Mixed Conditionals)
Exercise 16 — Open writing.

In 80–120 words, write a reflective paragraph about a pivotal past decision. Use the third conditional and at least one mixed conditional (past condition → present result, or vice versa), in a formal/literary register.
