Countable & Uncountable Nouns

Some, Any, Much, Many & A Lot Of

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

Countable nouns can be counted one by one and have a plural form. Uncountable nouns refer to a mass, substance, or idea and have no plural.

Type Feature Example
Countable has singular/plural; takes a/an in singular a book, two books, three chairs
Uncountable no plural; never a/an; takes singular verb water, information, advice, money
Units of measurement make uncountables countable: a piece of information, a glass of water, a bit of advice, a loaf of bread.

Quantifiers

✅ Affirmative
some (both types): I have some apples / some rice.
❌ Negative / ❓ Questions
any (both types): I don't have any money. Is there any milk?
🔢 Many
countable only: How many books do you have?
🧪 Much
uncountable only: How much time do we have?
➕ A lot of / lots of
both types, mainly affirmative: a lot of friends, a lot of money.
🔹 A few / a little
a few (countable), a little (uncountable): a few coins, a little sugar.
⚡ Few vs. a few / little vs. a little
A few / a little

Positive: "I have a few friends" (some, enough).

Few / little

Negative: "I have few friends" (almost none, not enough).

Watch out: some nouns look uncountable but aren't, and vice versa — "news," "furniture," "advice," and "information" are always uncountable in English, even though their translations may be countable in other languages.
