Reformulation Practice

Building Speaking Fluency

📘 1. Understanding the Skill

Reformulation means restating an idea in different words while keeping the same meaning. It builds fluency in two ways:

🔁 Corrective reformulation (recast)
A listener repeats what was said, fixing errors naturally, without breaking the flow: "I go to the doctor yesterday." → "Oh, you went to the doctor yesterday? What happened?"
💪 Self-reformulation (fluency drill)
The speaker restates their own idea 2–3 different ways, building flexibility under time pressure.
Key idea: reformulation is not the same as explicit error correction. It models the correct form inside a natural reply, so the conversation keeps moving and the speaker doesn't feel embarrassed.

LEVEL B2 · Upper-Intermediate
Exercise 10 — Read, then mark each statement T or F.

Reformulation is widely used in fluency-focused speaking practice, distinct from accuracy-focused error correction. Rather than stopping a speaker mid-sentence to fix a mistake, a listener reformulates the idea naturally in their response, modelling correct or more sophisticated language without breaking the flow of communication. Some speaking coaches also use self-reformulation drills, in which learners repeat a single idea two or three times, each time with different vocabulary or sentence structure, to build mental flexibility under time pressure — a skill particularly valuable in unscripted, spontaneous speech such as interviews or debates.

  1. Reformulation typically interrupts the speaker to correct them directly.
  2. Reformulation models correct language within a natural response.
  3. Self-reformulation drills involve repeating an idea with different wording.
  4. This skill is especially useful for spontaneous speech like interviews.
  5. Reformulation is the same as accuracy-focused error correction.
