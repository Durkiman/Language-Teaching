Gerunds & Infinitives

Verb + -ing or to + Verb

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

A gerund (verb-ing) acts like a noun; an infinitive (to + base verb) expresses purpose or a following action. Some verbs are followed by one, the other, or either — sometimes with a change in meaning.

Pattern Use Example
Verb + gerund enjoy, avoid, finish, suggest, mind I enjoy reading.
Verb + infinitive want, decide, hope, plan, promise I want to leave.
Verb + either start, begin, like, love, hate, continue It started raining / to rain.
Subject position: a gerund can also be the subject of a sentence — "Swimming is good exercise" (not "To swim is..." though this is possible in formal style).

After prepositions and "to"

🔗 After a preposition
always gerund: good at cooking, interested in learning.
🎯 To as infinitive marker
I want to go, she needs to rest.
⚠️ To as a preposition
gerund follows: I look forward to seeing you (not "to see").
🏗️ Verb + object + infinitive
want/tell/ask + someone + to do: I want you to stay.
⚡ Meaning changes: stop, remember, try
+ gerund

I stopped smoking. (quit the habit)

+ infinitive

I stopped to smoke. (paused another activity in order to smoke)

Remember/forget: + gerund = a past action ("I remember locking the door" = I recall doing it). + infinitive = a future obligation ("Remember to lock the door" = don't forget to do it).

Exercise 14 — Multiple choice: which meaning does the verb pattern express here?
  1. "He stopped to answer the phone." → a) paused another action  b) quit answering phones
  2. "I regret to inform you..." → a) a formal announcement about to be made  b) regret over a past action
  3. "I regret telling him the truth." → a) regret over a past action  b) an announcement about to be made
  4. "She tried restarting the router." → a) experimented with a solution  b) made an effort generally
  5. "He went on to become CEO." → a) a subsequent achievement  b) continuing the same action
  6. "He went on talking for hours." → a) continuing the same action  b) a subsequent achievement
