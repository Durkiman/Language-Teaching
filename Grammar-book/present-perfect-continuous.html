Present Perfect Continuous

Duration, Recent Activity & Present Evidence

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

Present perfect continuous (have/has been + verb-ing) connects the past to now, but focuses on the activity itself — how long it's been going on, or the visible evidence it's left behind.

duration up to now
I've been studying for two hours.
recent activity, visible evidence
You're out of breath — have you been running?
temporary, ongoing habit
She's been working from home lately.
emphasis / frustration
I've been waiting for an hour!
Careful: stative verbs don't take "-ing". I've known her for years. not I've been knowing her for years. Verbs like know, like, want, believe, own describe states, not activities.

Present perfect simple vs continuous

Simple — result / completionContinuous — duration / activity
I've painted the fence. (it's done)I've been painting the fence. (still going, or just stopped — look at the paint on my hands)
