Future Perfect & Future Continuous

Advanced Future Forms

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

Formation

Both tenses look forward from now to a point in the future, then describe what will be true at that point.

Tense Structure Example
Future Continuous will be + verb-ing This time tomorrow, I will be flying to Rome.
Future Perfect will have + past participle By 2030, I will have finished my degree.

When do we use each one?

⏳ Future Continuous — in progress at a future moment
At 8 p.m. tomorrow, I'll be cooking dinner.
✅ Future Perfect — completed before a future deadline
By Friday, she'll have finished the report.
🙏 Future Continuous — polite question about plans
Will you be using the car later?
📏 Future Perfect — duration up to a future point (+ for)
By next year, we'll have lived here for a decade.
⚡ In progress, or already done?
Future Continuous

Signal: at this time tomorrow / this time next week — the action is happening right then.

Future Perfect

Signal: by [time] / by the time / before — the action is finished before then.

Bonus (advanced): the future perfect continuous (will have been + -ing) emphasises duration up to a future point: By June, I will have been working here for five years.

Exercise 14 — Multiple choice: what is expressed here?
  1. "He'll have left already, I imagine." a) a confident assumption of completion  b) a scheduled event
  2. "Will you be needing anything else, sir?" a) a polite inquiry  b) a prediction
  3. "By the time help arrives, the situation will have deteriorated." a) completion before a future point  b) an ongoing action
  4. "The delegates will be arriving throughout the morning." a) a scheduled sequence  b) a polite request
  5. "I'll have finished by six, all being well." a) a projected completion, with slight uncertainty  b) a firm promise
  6. "This time next year, the merger will have gone through." a) completion before a specified point  b) an action in progress
