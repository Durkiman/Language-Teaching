Telling Time

What Time Is It?

📘 1. Understanding the Rule

English has two common ways to say a clock time. Both are correct — choose the one that fits the situation.

Digital style
hour + minutes, read like a number: 3:15 → "three fifteen"
Clock-face style
past / to the hour: 3:15 → "quarter past three"
TimeClock-face styleDigital style
3:00three o'clockthree
3:05five past threethree oh five
3:15quarter past threethree fifteen
3:30half past threethree thirty
3:45quarter to fourthree forty-five
3:50ten to fourthree fifty
Careful: "half three" ≠ "half past three" in every language. In English, half past three (and the informal short form half three) both mean 3:30. But in German, Dutch and several other languages, the equivalent phrase ("halb drei" / "half drie") means 2:30 — half to three, not half past it. If in doubt, say "half past three" in full.
⏰ Two clocks: 12-hour vs 24-hour

Everyday spoken English uses the 12-hour clock with a.m./p.m. Timetables, official schedules and much of Europe use the 24-hour clock — no a.m./p.m. needed.

9:00 a.m. → 09:00
1:00 p.m. → 13:00
6:30 p.m. → 18:30
midnight → 00:00
Careful: 12 a.m. / 12 p.m. "12 a.m." = midnight (00:00), "12 p.m." = midday/noon (12:00) — the numbers don't follow the usual a.m./p.m. pattern, so it's safer to just say "midnight" or "noon/midday."
